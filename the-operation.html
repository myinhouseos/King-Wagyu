Built on the right land, for the right reasons

King Wagyu is based on Farm Houmoed in the Otjozondjupa region of Namibia — a location chosen for what it offers a serious Wagyu program: space, climate stability, low disease pressure, and the ability to control herd environment from weaning through finishing.

Low stocking density allows natural behaviour and minimises stress-related cortisol responses that directly affect marbling deposition. The dry Namibian climate reduces parasite burden and supports the health consistency that premium production requires. Otjozondjupa's isolation from high-density cattle areas creates a biosecurity environment that supports the export health certifications required by international markets.

DeBruin and Bianca Nienaber reside on Farm Houmoed and oversee all day-to-day operational matters — ensuring that every protocol designed for the program is executed on the ground, every day.

Herd Management

Six principles that govern every animal, every day

A Wagyu operation is not managed like a conventional beef operation. The animals are different, the production timeline is different, and the commercial stakes of getting it wrong are different. These are the principles that drive daily decisions at King Wagyu.

01

Minimal Stress Handling

Wagyu are sensitive animals. Cortisol responses to poor handling directly affect marbling deposition. Every interaction — from routine veterinary work to weighing — follows low-stress protocols as a production standard, not a preference.

02

Nutritional Precision

Feed programmes are designed per phase of production — backgrounding, pre-finishing, and finishing — with transitions managed carefully to avoid the digestive disruption that affects weight gain and marbling. There is no single ration applied across the herd.

03

Individual Performance Tracking

Each animal is tracked from birth through slaughter or export. Growth rates, health events, condition scores, and parentage are recorded. This data drives selection decisions and allows problems to be identified before they become production losses.

04

Recipient Cow Standards

The embryo program's recipients are managed to the same standard as the donor herd. Body condition at transfer, synchronisation accuracy, and post-transfer monitoring are not afterthoughts — they are determinants of conception rate and foetal development quality.

05

Cohort Integrity

Animals are grouped by age and genetic line and remain in those cohorts through the finishing phase. Mixing of cohorts — which introduces social stress and disrupts feeding hierarchy — is avoided as a matter of management policy.

06

Documentation at Every Stage

Pedigree registration, DNA parentage verification, health records, and production data are maintained from birth. For live export and embryo sales, this documentation is the product — incomplete records mean reduced commercial value, regardless of animal quality.

Production Protocols

How we maintain quality across every cohort

Consistency is designed in, not hoped for. These are the operational protocols that translate genetics into reliable, grade-consistent results.

1

Breeding Selection

Sires are selected against documented EBVs for marbling, structure, temperament, and growth efficiency. No animal enters the breeding program without verified pedigree and performance data. Selection decisions are documented and reviewed against herd production outcomes each cycle.

2

Embryo Flush & Transfer

Donors are superovulated on a managed cycle. Flush timing, recipient synchronisation, and transfer procedures follow veterinary protocols developed for Wagyu-specific requirements. Embryo quality is graded and recorded. Conception rates are tracked per flush cycle and inform recipient management adjustments.

3

Weaning & Backgrounding

Weaning is managed to minimise stress. Animals are backgrounded in cohort groups with nutritional transition managed over a defined period before pre-finishing begins. Bodyweight and condition targets at each phase are set against finishing timeline requirements, not arbitrary calendar dates.

4

Finishing Protocol

Time on feed is set per cohort based on genetic profile and target grade distribution. Animals are not pulled early to free up feedlot space. The finishing phase is treated as the final investment in a multi-year production cycle — shortchanging it is not an operational option.

5

Health & Biosecurity

A standing health protocol covers vaccination schedules, parasite management, and biosecurity entry requirements for the property. For animals destined for live export or export processing, health management begins with birth records and is maintained to meet the importing country's specific veterinary requirements.

6

Export Documentation

Pedigree registration, DNA verification, health certification, and classification paperwork are prepared in parallel with production — not assembled after the fact. The documentation chain is treated as a production output with the same quality standards applied to the animals themselves.

The Namibia Advantage

Why Namibia is the right base for this operation

Namibia is not the obvious choice for a Wagyu operation. That is, in part, why it is the right one. The competitive concentration of Wagyu producers in Australia, the US, and Japan creates market friction — supply abundance, price compression, and input cost escalation. Building from Namibia creates a different position entirely.

✦

Disease-Free Status

Namibia maintains FMD-free zones recognised by international veterinary bodies. This opens export health certification pathways that are unavailable to producers in higher-risk regions.

✦

Emerging Market Position

As a Southern African Wagyu source, King Wagyu operates in a market with minimal direct competition. Early positioning in an emerging supply region creates advantages that become more pronounced as demand grows.

✦

Production Cost Structure

Land, feed, and operational costs in Namibia create a production economics profile distinct from saturated Wagyu markets. This creates room to invest in quality while maintaining export price competitiveness.

✦

African Continent Gateway

As Wagyu demand grows across Middle Eastern and African premium markets, proximity and regional relationships create supply chain advantages that international competitors cannot easily replicate.
